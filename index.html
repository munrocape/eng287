Interactive texts

"Interactive [text] is a method of communication in which the [text]'s outputs depend on the user's inputs, and the user's inputs in turn affect the [text]'s outputs" (Investopedia, 2016).

Types of interactivity

Ryan outlines two types of interactivity - interaction facilitated by the medium itself as well as the work. She gives the example of television as a slightly interactive medium - a user has the option to change channels as they see fit (Ryan, 205). Television shows themselves range in interactivity - an example of one of the most types of interactive programs are those where users can directly interact with and influence the show like the Washington Journal - individuals can call in and speak directly with the hosts and guests.

Dora the Explorer is a children's program where the characters often speaks directly to the viewer and encourages them to interact with the program - point out clues to puzzles, stand up and move around, as well as other tasks. While a viewer will not directly influence the program, there are planned lapses and pauses that allow a viewer to reach their own conclusions before the results are revealed.

Luther is an example of a non-interactive program - these are the dominating form of programs as they are devoid of any user interaction and just present a story.


Levels of interactivity

"Types of interactivity can also be distinguised on the basis of the freedom granted to the user and the degree of intentionality of his interventions" (Ryan, 205). Ryan provides an example for a reactive work - one which the viewer cannot directly influence but still impacts - as an art installation that may react to the noise level in a room. Reactive Sound & Light Art Installation, by students at Washington State University, utilized motion sensors to trigger sound and video projections.

The next level of interactivity is a random selection amongst many alternatives that is triggered by the user. Consider an origami fortune teller - a papercraft where the user selects arbitrary numbers and colors to reveal one panel and subsequently their "fortune". Ryan provides the example of randomly clicking hyperlinks (Ryan, 205).

Finally, the most interactive a text can be is when a user "leaves a durable mark on the textual world, either by adding objects to its landscape or by writing its history" (Ryan, 205). Consider video games, where users can save their progress and shape their world like in Minecraft - a game where users have full reign over shaping the landscape - or a coloring book.



Electronic texts

Any text that is broadcast digitally (Ryan, 209). However, it is worth noting that electronic texts extend beyond just textual content. Ryan includes a number of features which will be explored below.

Exclusive features of electronic texts

While there are many features of texts that can be replicated in non-electronic and electronic formats, Ryan notes that there are certain features that are much more accessible in a electronic format.

  • Mobility of text: text comes to the user through a display, instead of a user reaching out for a text
  • Kaleidoscopic: text can be easily rearranged - an example being how text of this page changes on button click
  • Algorithmic: text is controlled via logic programs that are not readily viewable by the reader
  • Hypermediacy: text can be expressed in a variety of means - through videos, sounds, and text - hypermedia.
  • Interrupt Structure: text can be explored in a variety of orders instead of the usual front to back - links on a page can skip the regular progression and flow of reading.
  • Animation: visual works provide additional context and understanding that can be difficult to replicate in only text.
  • Temporal Exploration: once a non-electronic text is aquired, barring destruction, it will always be there. The same cannot be said for an electronic text - time can impact what is presented to the user.
- (Ryan, 214-217)

Ergodic texts

"A class of works in which "non-trivial" effort is required to allow the reader to traverse the text" (Ryan, 206).

Difference between interactive works

Ergodic texts may appear to be similar to interactive works - they require effort by the reader to progress. These may seem similar to a progressive video game, however, Ryan distinguishes them further by stating that "ergodic design is a built-in reading protocol involving a feedback loop that enables the text to modify itself, so that a reader will encounter different sequences of signs during different reading sessions" (Ryan, 206).

Feedback loop

Thus, there is a clear component to ergodic works - the feedback loop - that add an additional component to the work. Ryan separates the text into three layers: the text that is written, the text that is read, as well as the constructed model of the text in the reader's head (Ryan, 207).

Examples

Applying the definition of requiring non-trivial effort, a number of examples exist. Noah Wardrip-Fruin gives the example of ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs - sentences would be scrambled with one another and decoding would require to unravel the work (Wardrip-Fruin). Modern examples include video games like TinyMUD, a textual video game, and House of Leaves, a novel by Mark Z. Danielewski. Both of these modern examples can be read linearly but as a user interacts with and traverses the text it takes on new meaning.
